Lecture 1 – Introduction to Quantitative Methods

What are Quantitative Methods? Definition ▼

Quantitative methods involve the process of collecting, analyzing, and interpreting numerical data to understand phenomena and make predictions. They provide a structured approach to studying relationships, patterns, and trends within datasets.

Importance of Quantitative Methods (4 Reasons) Key Concept ▼
  • Objectivity – minimizes bias and subjectivity by relying on numerical data and statistical analyses.
  • Precision and Accuracy – enables precise measurement and accurate representation for reliable conclusions.
  • Generalizability – findings can often be generalized to larger populations beyond the sampled data.
  • Predictive Power – identifies patterns and relationships that allow for predictions and forecasting of future outcomes.
Key Components of Quantitative Methods Types ▼
  • Research Design – formulating research questions, selecting methodologies, designing experiments or surveys.
  • Data Collection – gathering data through surveys, experiments, observations, or secondary sources.
  • Statistical Analysis – applying descriptive and inferential statistical techniques to the data.
  • Interpretation & Reporting – communicating findings in context through reports, presentations, or visualizations.
Common Quantitative Methods Types ▼
  • Descriptive Statistics – mean, median, mode, standard deviation; summarize data distributions and central tendencies.
  • Inferential Statistics – hypothesis testing, regression analysis, ANOVA; draw conclusions about populations from sample data.
  • Experimental Design – manipulates independent variables to observe effects on dependent variables while controlling extraneous factors.
  • Survey Research – questionnaires or interviews to understand attitudes, behaviors, and opinions within a population.
Challenges and Considerations Key Concept ▼
  • Sampling Bias – samples must be representative of the population to avoid biased results.
  • Data Quality – accuracy, reliability, and validity of data are essential for meaningful conclusions.
  • Ethical Considerations – researchers must ensure participant confidentiality and informed consent.
  • Interpretation Limitations – interpretations should acknowledge uncertainties and assumptions.

Lecture 2 – Overview of Statistics

What is Statistics & Data? Definition ▼

Data – information from observations, counts, measurements, or responses.

Statistics – the science of collecting, organizing, analyzing, and interpreting data to make decisions.

Population vs. Sample Definition ▼
  • Population – the entire collection of outcomes, responses, or measurements of interest.
  • Sample – a subset of the population.

Example: 250 college students surveyed out of all students → the 250 are the sample; all students are the population.

Parameter vs. Statistic Key Concept ▼
  • Parameter – numerical description of a population characteristic.
  • Statistic – numerical description of a sample characteristic.

Memory trick: Parameter → Population  |  Statistic → Sample

Descriptive vs. Inferential Statistics Types ▼
  • Descriptive – organizes, summarizes, and displays data (charts, tables, averages).
  • Inferential – uses a sample to draw conclusions about a population.
Types of Data: Qualitative & Quantitative Types ▼
  • Qualitative – non-numerical; categories/labels (e.g., gender, color, name).
  • Quantitative – numerical measurements or counts (e.g., GPA, weight, age).

Independent vs. Dependent Variables Definition ▼
  • Independent Variable – causes or influences the other variable; can be manipulated.
  • Dependent Variable – the outcome; changes in response to the independent variable.
Sampling Techniques Types ▼
  • Stratified – members from each segment (e.g., freshmen, sophomores, etc.).
  • Cluster – all members from randomly selected groups (e.g., city blocks).
  • Systematic – every nth member selected after a random start.
  • Convenience – uses only readily available members.

Lecture 3 – Statistics (Quantitative Methods)

What is Statistics? (Lecture 3 definition) Definition ▼

Statistics involves using sample data to predict, estimate, and support managerial decision-making. It refers to scientific methods by which data are collected, organized, presented, and analyzed.

A statistic is a number calculated from sample data describing a characteristic of that sample. A parameter is a number calculated from population data.

Purposes of Statistics Key Concept ▼
  • To summarize findings
  • To visualize results
  • To extract information from data
  • To communicate the findings
Qualitative vs. Quantitative Variables (Lecture 3) Types ▼
  • Quantitative – numerical values (further split into discrete and continuous).
  • Qualitative – non-numerical observations representing categories.
  • Discrete – countable whole numbers (e.g., number of students).
  • Continuous – measurable, can take any value in a range (e.g., height, temperature).
Representative Sample & Random Sampling Definition ▼

A representative sample accurately reflects the population it is drawn from. It requires random sampling, which ensures every subset has the same chance of being included.

Lecture 4 – Statistical Data

Sources of Data: Primary vs. Secondary Definition ▼
  • Primary Data – collected directly from the source (e.g., salaries obtained from the HR department or payroll office).
  • Secondary Data – obtained from secondary sources such as books, journals, newspapers, magazines, and prior research studies.
5 Methods of Gathering Data Types ▼
  • Survey – pen-and-paper or web-based questionnaires; can yield both qualitative and quantitative data.
  • Interview – commonly collects qualitative data; conducted face-to-face, by telephone, or via digital platforms.
  • Observation – records the dynamics of events or situations, and frequency of specific behaviors or attributes.
  • Experimentation – controlled study to understand cause-and-effect relationships between variables.
  • Review of Documents/Records – data gathered from minutes of meetings, attendance records, written reports, and financial records.
Data Representation: Organize → Present → Interpret Key Concept ▼

Raw data must be organized and presented using tabular and graphical methods before it can be properly interpreted.

The three steps: Organize Data → Present Data → Interpret Data

Frequency Distribution Table (Ungrouped & Grouped) Key Concept ▼

A frequency distribution shows how often each value or class occurs.

Steps for Grouped Data:

  • Find the Range (R) = Highest value − Lowest value.
  • Find the Class Interval (I) = R ÷ number of desired classes (round to nearest whole number).
  • Set up classes starting from the lowest; starting point must be divisible by the interval.
  • Tally data into each class and count the frequency (f).
  • Compute the Class Midpoint = (Lower limit + Upper limit) ÷ 2.

Example: Salaries ₱23,000–₱43,000 → R = 20,000; 5 classes → I = 4,000.

How to Interpret Graphs Example ▼
  • Line Graph: Identify years of marked increase/decrease, peak points, and future trends.
  • Bar Graph: Identify which category is highest/lowest and compare groups.
  • Histogram: Identify which class range has the highest frequency and describe distribution shape.
  • Pie Chart: Express each slice as a percentage and rank categories from largest to smallest.

Key Example: Enrollment peaked in 2015 at ~1,700 then declined, suggesting external influences on enrollment rates.
